Our mission

Our mission is to support and raise awareness about child exploitation (fire-breathing kids) in Vietnam who are living under dangerous conditions. We created this website to help you understand what goes behind these ‘exciting’ performances that you might not know about. Throughout this website, we will provide simple and clear information about the risks, challenges that these kids face, and how you can deal with these situations; guidance on what to do, and how you can help support our mission. We aim to encourage thoughtful, helpful, and responsible actions from the public, so that these children are not harmed. We hope to contribute to our mission of supporting and building a better environment where we can protect their well-being.

Stories

Story No. 1

Thanh has already seen plenty of ups and downs on the streets of Ho Chi Minh City—many of which have tested his patience and his hope. One story that sticks with him is that of a boy he found in distress on a busy street one evening. “When I went there and spoke with the children performing on the streets to entertain tourists, one of them was crying. The others urged me to help him. They said he had been beaten by their ‘boss’—the person who controlled the money they earned. The boy, Hoang, told me he didn’t want to stay. I said I could take him somewhere safe, and he immediately agreed. I decided to trick the gang leader. I told him I was taking Hoang out to eat, then put him on my bike then drove him to another district. Hoang was terrified we were being followed, so I kept driving until we were far enough away for him to feel safe. Eventually, I brought him to his grandparents’ house. At first, his grandmother called the police, thinking I had taken him. But once I explained everything, they understood. Hoang now lives with his family again and has a job. He still needs support; his family’s situation isn’t fully stable, and he carries psychological effects from his time on the streets. But he is no longer alone, and Blue Dragon continues to support him.” Thanh, from Blue Dragon Children’s Foundation

Story No. 2

It was midnight at Bui Vien Street in the busiest area of HCMC. The street was full of tourists. Shops were still open as if it was daylight. They were shimmering with multi-colored LED lights. Tourists were dancing happily to loud music. In the middle of the crowd on the bustling street, flames flared up from time to time, which caught many people’s attention. They were coming from the street children, who were blowing fire to entertain travelers and earn money. Standing in the middle of the street, a girl introduced herself as Thy, born in 2010. She repeatedly covered her mouth with her hand after performing fire-breathing that astonished many tourists. Thy said she just learned how to blow fire a few months ago. She often leaves kerosene in her mouth. After fire-breathing, she needs to cover her mouth with her hands or absorb excessive oil with her shirt. Sometimes, Thy covers her mouth with painful burns. "I am a newcomer here and I took the job after my friends showed me how to hold kerosene in my mouth to blow fire. I am not really professional, but I always try to do my best and then walk around the town begging for money. If people feel sorry for me, they will give me money," she said.

Source: https://vietnamnet.vn/en/the-children-who-earn-a-living-by-blowing-fire-on-western-street-2192946.h…

Risks ignored

“Sometimes I keep the oil in my mouth for so long and I accidentally swallow it. At first, this made my stomach painful and uncomfortable, but I got used to it. I'm most afraid of burning my tongue while blowing the fire. Every time I get burned, my tongue blisters, and I have difficulty eating and drinking for several days," she said. Thy comes to Bui Vien Street every night and work until midnight, after travelers have left. Sometimes, Thy and her friends sleep under bridges, or on benches in the park. The job gives her around 300,000-400,000 VND each night. However, as Thy doesn’t have a tool set and has to perform fire blowing with borrowed tools, she has to share the revenue with lenders. So, she only gets to keep 200,000 VND a night only.

What risks do they face during these performances?

The substances used (usually kerosene or white spirit/mineral spirits) are hydrocarbons, and their effects on a developing child’s body are devastating.

Respiratory Destruction: “Fire-Breather’s Lung”

The most immediate severe risk is Chemical Pneumonia (also called Hydrocarbon Poisoning - Injuries and Poisoning”). What happens: When a child mists the fuel out of their mouth, tiny droplets can be accidentally inhaled (aspirated) into the lungs. The impact: These chemicals dissolve the protective lining of the lungs (surfactant), causing the lungs to fill with fluid and become inflamed. This can lead to Acute Respiratory Distress Syndrome (ARDS), where the body can no longer get enough oxygen to the blood (Saraf).

Source: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3481926/

Digestive and Gastrointestinal Damage

Since the children hold these fuels in their mouths, accidental swallowing is frequent. Chemical Burns: The fuel causes immediate burns to the mouth, throat, and esophagus (“Kerosene Poisoning - UF Health”). Stomach Ulcers: Constant ingestion of trace amounts of kerosene leads to chronic stomach pain, nausea, and severe peptic ulcers (“Kerosene Poisoning - UF Health”).

Source: https://ufhealth.org/conditions-and-treatments/kerosene-poisoning

Neurological & Systemic Poisoning

Hydrocarbons are absorbed through the skin of the mouth and the digestive tract into the bloodstream. Central Nervous System (CNS) Depression: Inhaling or swallowing these vapors can cause “fuel poisoning”, resulting in dizziness, headaches, confusion, and even seizures or comas (“Mineral Spirits Poisoning: MedlinePlus Medical Encyclopedia”). Organ Damage: Long-term exposure is linked to damage in the kidneys and liver, as these organs struggle to filter the toxic petrochemicals out of the child’s system (“Mineral Spirits Poisoning: MedlinePlus Medical Encyclopedia”).

Source: https://medlineplus.gov/ency/article/002798.htm

Long-term Cancer Risks

The fuel used on the street often has additives like benzene or sulfureted compounds. Carcinogenic Exposure: Benzene is a known human carcinogen. Frequent contact increases the risk of mouth, throat, and lung cancer later in life.

Source: https://zenartsla.com/modern-day-dragons-dangerous-secrets-fire-breathing/

What is happening behind the scenes that you don’t see?

Child Exploitation case revealed in Can Tho A recent investigation by the Criminal Police Department and Ninh Ward Police has exposed a disturbing reality behind the city's street performances.

On October 14, 2025, authorities dismantled a gang that exploits children for "fire-breathing" acts and street begging.

The Trap

The investigation centered on a man named Luom and his wife, Trang. Despite having no biological relationship with the children in their care, the couple allegedly "raised" them for the purpose of generating income. According to police reports, the operation was carefully organized: • The Schedule: Every night between 8:00 PM and 1:00 AM, Luom hired transporters to take the children to high-traffic tourist areas in Tan An, Ninh Kieu, Cai Khe, An Binh, and Hung Phu. • The Surveillance: Children were never truly alone. Luom’s family members acted as "supervisors," watching the children from a distance to make sure they performed and collected money. • The Cycle: After the performances ended, children were told to sit and wait until the next "shift," effectively keeping them in a cycle of work instead of going to school.

A Business of Exploitation

The financial breakdown of this operation reveals severe inequality. It is estimated that Luom and Trang made roughly 30 million VND per month from the children's labor. The children are given only 50,000 to 100,000 VND for snacks that are used as a small "reward" to keep them compliant.

Education vs. Survival

This case highlights a heartbreaking trend in Vietnam’s tourism places. Many children tricked into quitting their education because the immediate cash they earn feels more important than a degree. Whether forced by gangs or by extreme family poverty, these children are sacrificing their long-term health for money.

Why This Matters

When tourists tip a child fire-breather, they believe they are helping a hungry child. In reality: 1. You may be funding a gang: As seen in the Luom case, most of the money goes to gangs, not the child's well-being. 2. You are encouraging health risks: Fire-breathing causes permanent lung damage. If "exploitation business" is profitable, exploitation continues. 3. You are supporting the replacement of school with "work": Every dollar earned on the street is a reason for a child-exploiter to keep a child out of the classroom. Before you give, think.

Sources: Police investigation into child exploitation in Can Tho (Báo Nghệ An); How to help street children in Vietnam

Why raising awareness is important

In many local places in Vietnam, fire-breathing children are often seen as ‘exotic entertainment’ or ‘street culture’. Raising awareness is about shifting that perspective from entertainment to protection.

Breaking the cycle of demand

Tourists often give money out of admiration, not realizing that high earnings for a child performer can encourage traffickers to keep that child on the street instead of in school.

Hidden physical dangers

Fire-breathing is unacceptable hazardous. Even worse than burns, children often ingest ‘white spirit’ or kerosene, which can lead to chemical pneumonia, respiratory failure, and long-term organ damage.

Exposing the lack of agency

Awareness helps the public understand that these children are rarely there by choice. They are often controlled by child exploiters, or organized groups that takes most of the money that the child performers earn, leaving the child in a cycle of poverty and abuse.

Empowering the community

When tourists and locals are educated, they stop being ‘passive observers’ and become part of the solution by supporting NGOs rather than individual street performers.
